=== COMMENT 9 ===
The Computer Science CODA statistics are public. For the 2024-25 cycle, the middle 50% of accepted CSC students had GPAs from 3.43-3.97, meaning 25% were below 3.43. Out of 268 applicants, only 9 were denied (though most applicants are likely already competitive). If you keep your grades up and perform well, acceptance into CSC at NCSU is very likely. UNC tends to emphasize theory, while NCSU focuses more on practical, applied CS. Both universities will prepare you well so it mainly depends on the style of education you want. Note: the CODA stats are likely inflated due to selection bias — students are more likely to list CSC as their first choice only if they believe they are already competitive.
=== END COMMENT 9 ===

=== COMMENT 11 ===
Comp Sci class of 2020 here. I think State's Comp Sci program is great if you want to get a job and start working soon. State's Comp Sci program leans towards a lot more practicality over theory. You do take a few courses on data structures and algorithms, linear algebra, logic, etc. but many courses are very hands-on. By the time I graduated, I had worked with Java, C, C++, Assembly, Python, HTML/CSS/JavaScript, Groovy, and a few others. During those years, Chapel Hill focused mostly on just Java — they'd have a deeper understanding on a theoretical level, which is good for certain roles and getting a Masters, but State would have a more diverse skill set which made job hunting easier. As far as difficulty, the coursework is pretty intense. Since you're in the school of engineering, you have to meet engineering requirements too on top of all the CSC department requirements. The overall degree is something like 122 credit hours. How difficult internships will be really varies on your skill level and personal projects. I was able to get an internship as a freshman even though I had only taken Intro to Java, but I had done some small projects on the side. Cons: course work can be pretty intense — one semester I was basically alternating between tests and projects every week across my CSC courses. Some professors' teaching styles are very hit or miss. A few professors are professors since they like teaching, but a lot are professors since they're here for research.
=== END COMMENT 11 ===

=== COMMENT 13 ===
I'm a tech recruiter that has spent time at career fairs. NCSU is a well respected school with an aggressive departmental approach in terms of reaching out to companies to get them to hire their students for internships and after graduation. It's solidly in the "next tier" after the elite CS schools. The main thing about CS right now is you need to be thinking about AI and positioning yourself for the future. A CS degree is not like it was a few years ago where it was a ticket to a quick 6-figure salary and lots of jobs. Internships and work projects are more important than GPA. Companies want to know you have actually put things into action. The key is getting that initial experience anywhere and then building on it — don't think you can only intern at Microsoft. Find small companies that can't afford to pay programmers big salaries and do work for them inexpensively, then use that experience to leverage better jobs.
=== END COMMENT 13 ===
